ISO 8501-1 Rust Grade Definitions for Uncoated Steel Surfaces

This document describes the four rust grades defined by the international standard ISO 8501-1, which is used worldwide to assess the condition of steel surfaces before coating or remediation. These grades are assigned by visual inspection and describe the extent of mill scale and rust present on the metal surface.

Grade A describes a steel surface that is completely covered with adherent mill scale, with little or no rust visible. The surface has not yet begun to deteriorate significantly and no pitting is present. A Grade A surface is considered to be in excellent condition and requires only standard surface preparation before coating.

Grade B describes a steel surface that has begun to rust, with some mill scale that has started to flake away. Rust is visible on the surface but has not penetrated deeply into the metal. The surface shows early signs of corrosion but structural integrity is generally not yet compromised. Grade B surfaces require thorough cleaning and rust removal before any protective coating is applied.

Grade C describes a steel surface from which the mill scale has rusted away entirely or can be scraped off. Rust is visible over the entire surface, and there may be slight pitting visible to the naked eye. A Grade C surface indicates significant corrosion that has progressed beyond surface level. Structural assessment is recommended before continued service, and immediate protective treatment is required.

Grade D describes a steel surface on which the mill scale has rusted away and extensive pitting is visible to the naked eye. The surface shows severe corrosion with deep pits that indicate material loss. A Grade D surface presents serious concerns about structural integrity and fitness for service. Engineering assessment is mandatory, and the component may require replacement depending on its load-bearing role.

The rust grades from A to D represent a progressive deterioration scale. Grade A is the mildest condition and Grade D is the most severe. For any component in active service, the presence of Grade C or Grade D corrosion should trigger an immediate engineering review to determine whether the component remains safe for its intended use.
